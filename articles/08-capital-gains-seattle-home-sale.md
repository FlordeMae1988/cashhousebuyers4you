# Capital Gains Tax on a Seattle Home Sale in 2026 — 4 Strategies That Work in Today's Slow Market

**Seattle homeowners who bought 5+ years ago are facing capital gains bills of $50,000-$300,000+ when they sell. Most sellers don't know there are legal strategies to dramatically reduce or defer that tax. In today's slower market, these strategies matter even more — because every $10k the market has dropped compounds with every $10k in tax you could have saved with the right structure. This guide covers 4 strategies that actually work, all perfectly legal, most dramatically underused.**

**Important disclaimer:** This is educational, not tax advice. Every situation is different. **Talk to a CPA or tax attorney before any sale.** We're not tax professionals — we're home buyers who structure sales creatively. But we've structured these strategies dozens of times.

## Why Today's Slow Market Makes Tax Planning More Important

Three reasons why 2026 is a particularly bad time to make tax mistakes on a Seattle sale:

**1. Market values are lower, so every dollar matters more.** If your Bellevue home was worth $1.2M in 2021 and $1.0M today, you've already lost $200k of potential sale price. Losing another $80k to unnecessary taxes compounds the problem.

**2. Slower sales mean more time for strategy.** In 2021's hot market, you listed and sold in a week — no time to think about tax structure. In today's market where homes sit 4-6+ months, you have time to structure correctly.

**3. Creative structures (seller financing, installment sales) work better when cash buyers are being more conservative.** Fewer cash buyers = more room for alternative structures that also happen to deliver tax advantages.

## The Problem: How Much You'll Actually Owe

**Example — Ballard homeowner (single filer):**
- Bought in 2014 for $465,000
- Selling in 2026 for $880,000 (realistic given market slowdown from peak)
- **Gain: $415,000**
- Section 121 primary residence exclusion (single): -$250,000
- Taxable gain: $165,000
- Long-term federal capital gains tax: ~$25,000-$33,000

**Same home, married filing jointly:**
- Exclusion: -$500,000
- Taxable gain: $0
- **Federal tax: $0**

**Same home but it's a RENTAL (no primary residence exclusion):**
- Taxable gain: $415,000
- Plus depreciation recapture (if rental): significant additional tax
- Tax bill: **$80,000-$130,000+**

**Washington state:** No income tax on primary residence sale. Note: Washington's 7% capital gains tax (enacted 2022) does NOT apply to real estate — only certain investment gains. You're clear on that front.

## Strategy 1: Primary Residence Exclusion (Section 121)

The easy $500,000 (married) or $250,000 (single) exclusion.

**Requirements:**
- Owned the home **2 of the last 5 years**
- Lived in the home as primary residence **2 of the last 5 years**
- Haven't used the exclusion on another property in the last 2 years

**If you qualify and your gain is under the threshold, straight cash sale usually makes sense** — you're already tax-free within the exclusion. Over the threshold? See Strategy 2.

**Partial exclusion** available if you're selling due to:
- Job change
- Health issues
- Unforeseen circumstances (divorce, death in family, natural disaster)

**Timing matters.** If you're close to the 2-year mark, waiting a few weeks could make or break eligibility. Talk to your CPA.

## Strategy 2: Installment Sale via Seller Financing (HUGE in Today's Market)

**This is the most dramatically underused strategy for Seattle sellers in 2026.**

When you sell via seller financing (receive payments over time), capital gains are **spread across years** — not realized all at once.

### Why It's Especially Powerful Now

In 2021's hot market, straight cash sales at peak prices looked great on paper — but the tax hit was brutal. Many Seattle sellers walked away with 20-30% less than they thought.

In 2026's slower market, prices are lower AND the tax implications of a lump-sum sale are higher (relative to the gain). Seller financing shines in this environment.

### The Math Example

**Same $880k Ballard rental property, $415k gain:**

**Option A — Straight cash sale:**
- Gain realized: $415,000 in 2026
- Federal tax (24% bracket, windfall): ~$100,000
- Plus depreciation recapture (for rental): ~$25,000
- **Total federal tax: $125,000 in 2026**
- Plus loss of 2.8% mortgage to current 7% rates if seller had one

**Option B — Seller financing over 20 years:**
- Down payment: $175k in 2026 + $4,200/month for 20 years at 7%
- Total collected: $1,178,000 over 20 years
- Gain recognized PRO RATA on principal payments — not all at once
- Effective tax rate much lower (staying in lower brackets each year): ~15% effective
- **Total federal tax: ~$65,000 spread over 20 years**
- **Savings: $60,000** plus much better cash flow

### Who Should Consider Seller Financing

- **Sellers with large gains** (any Seattle investment property purchased before 2018)
- **Sellers in high income brackets** (avoiding the bracket jump in year of sale)
- **Retirees who want monthly income** more than lump sum
- **Estate planning situations** (note can be inherited)
- **Sellers of investment properties** (no primary residence exclusion available)

### Caveats

- Depreciation recapture (for rentals) is typically recognized in year of sale — still a big hit
- Interest is ordinary income, not capital gains
- You still need a buyer willing to finance (we often are)
- Not recommended for sellers who urgently need a lump sum

## Strategy 3: 1031 Exchange (Investment Properties Only)

If the Seattle home is a **rental or investment property** (not your primary residence), you can defer capital gains entirely by reinvesting via 1031 exchange.

**Rules (highly summarized):**
- Must be investment property on both ends
- **45 days to identify** replacement property
- **180 days to close**
- Use a **qualified intermediary** (required)
- Replacement must be equal-or-greater value and debt

**Why it's underused:**
- Tight timing windows
- Requires another investment property purchase
- Many Seattle sellers want to cash out, not redeploy

**When to use:** You want to stay invested in real estate but exit Seattle or change property type. Popular for sellers moving capital to Texas, Florida, Arizona markets with better cash flow.

## Strategy 4: Opportunity Zone Investment

Invest capital gains into a **Qualified Opportunity Fund** to defer federal capital gains:
- Defer tax until fund exit OR 2026 (current deadline — check current law)
- **10+ year hold:** Gains from the Opportunity Zone investment itself are tax-free

**Complex.** Requires working with a Qualified Opportunity Fund. Less commonly used but can work for sellers of highly appreciated Seattle properties who want to redeploy into long-term investment.

## Combining Strategies (Often Best)

The best Seattle sellers in today's market often combine strategies:

- **Primary residence exclusion + seller financing** — take $500k exclusion upfront, finance the remainder over time
- **1031 exchange + partial cash-out** — use 1031 for investment portion, take cash for excluded portion
- **Seller financing + periodic refinance rights** — structure with refinance options that give flexibility

**This is where a good CPA is invaluable.** The $2,000-$3,000 you spend on CPA consultation saves $50,000+ in taxes. Highest-ROI decision before selling.

## Washington's Capital Gains Tax — Doesn't Hit Real Estate

Washington implemented a **7% tax on long-term capital gains above ~$262,000** (indexed annually) in 2022. **Does NOT apply to:**
- Real estate sales
- Retirement account withdrawals
- Small business sales under certain conditions

**So home sellers don't pay Washington state capital gains on the sale itself.** But if you have other stock gains, the combined picture matters. CPA check: yes.
