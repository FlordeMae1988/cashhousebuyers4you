## Florida Probate Basics

Florida probate is governed by **Florida Statutes Chapter 733**.

**Process:**

1. **Petition for Administration** filed in the county where deceased lived (Hernando or Pasco County Circuit Court)
2. **Letters of Administration** issued to personal representative (Florida's term for executor)
3. **Notice to creditors** for 3 months
4. **Debts paid, inventory filed**
5. **Final Order of Distribution**

**Typical Florida probate timeline: 6-10 months.**

## Florida Homestead Exemption — Critical for Heirs

If the deceased was using the property as primary residence (homestead), it has special protections that affect inheritance:

- **Homestead protected from most creditors** — most debts of the deceased can't force the sale
- **Descent rules apply** — Florida statute may override the will for homestead property in certain cases (particularly when there's a surviving spouse + minor children)
- **Property tax portability** — if an heir moves into the homestead, they may preserve the "Save Our Homes" tax cap

**Practical impact:** If deceased had homestead, you may have cleaner selling options than if it was investment property. Get a Florida probate attorney.

## Step-Up Basis — Florida Tax Impact

**Your cost basis** = fair market value on date of death (not what the deceased paid).

**Florida has no state income tax.** So no state-level capital gains concerns on the sale.

**Federal capital gains** applies to the gain above the date-of-death value. Get a Florida-licensed appraisal within 60-90 days of death to lock in your basis.

**In today's slow market, the step-up basis often means estates sell at a LOSS for tax purposes** (sale price is below the date-of-death appraisal), which can offset other gains.
